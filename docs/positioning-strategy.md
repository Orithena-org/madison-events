# Madison Events: Positioning Strategy Analysis

**Date:** 2026-02-26
**Status:** Draft for review

---

## The Core Tension

You want two things that seem to conflict:

1. **Personal utility** — A product that reflects your taste (tech/outdoor/music)
2. **Revenue potential** — A business that can actually make money in a city of 270K

This analysis argues they're not in conflict at all. The solution is **voice-driven curation, not category restriction**.

---

## Competitive Landscape

### Who You're Up Against

| Player | Type | Audience | Strength | Weakness |
|--------|------|----------|----------|----------|
| **608today** (6AM City) | Daily newsletter | 33K+ subs, general | Massive reach, VC-backed, AI-scaled | Generic tone, covers everything, no personality |
| **Isthmus** | Alt-weekly + calendar | Broad Madison | Deep editorial, comprehensive listings | Old media format, cluttered calendar |
| **Channel3000** | TV station events | Mainstream | Institutional trust | Events are afterthought to news |
| **VisitMadison** | Tourism board | Visitors | Official, comprehensive | Tourist-facing, not for locals |
| **Eventbrite** | Platform | Event creators | Self-serve listings | No curation, no local voice |

### The Gap in the Market

Every existing player is either **comprehensive-but-generic** or **institutional-but-stale**. Nobody in Madison is doing what Morning Brew did for finance news or The Infatuation did for restaurants: **covering a broad category through a specific, recognizable voice that people actually want in their inbox**.

608today is the closest competitor, but their model is deliberately neutral — 6AM City's playbook explicitly avoids politics and personality. Their $10 ARPU at scale is fine for a VC-backed company running 30+ markets. You don't need their scale; you need their engagement at a fraction of the subscriber count.

## Three Positioning Options

### Option A: Full Niche — "Outdoor + Music Events Only"

**What it looks like:** Only cover hiking, biking, paddling, trail runs, live music, DJ sets, music festivals. Ignore everything else.

**Pros:**
- Ultra-clear brand identity
- Attracts very specific sponsors (outdoor gear, music venues, breweries)
- High engagement from the exact right people
- Easy content curation (binary: is it outdoor/music? yes or no)

**Cons:**
- Madison metro is ~680K people. The outdoor + live music overlap is maybe 15-30K addressable
- Some weeks you'd have 3 events to share. Other weeks, 40. Inconsistent.
- Sponsors are limited to a narrow category (REI, local bike shops, venues)
- Caps your ceiling at maybe 3-5K highly engaged subscribers
- You personally would miss events you'd actually want to attend

**Revenue ceiling:** $3-5K/month at maturity (1-3 years). Niche CPMs ($50-70) offset small list size, but sponsor pool is shallow.

---

### Option B: Go Broad — "Everything in Madison"

**What it looks like:** Comprehensive events calendar. All categories, no filter, compete with 608today and Isthmus on coverage.

**Pros:**
- Maximum addressable market
- Any local business could sponsor
- Never run out of content
- Easier to hit subscriber milestones for ad networks

**Cons:**
- Directly competes with 608today (33K subs, VC-backed, AI-scaled to 350+ markets)
- No differentiation — why would someone switch from 608today to you?
- Generic voice = commodity product = race to the bottom on CPMs
- You wouldn't even enjoy building it
- Your scraper already pulls the same data Isthmus shows — zero added value

**Revenue ceiling:** Either $0 (can't compete) or $10-15K/month if you somehow overtake 608today (you won't).

---

### Option C: The Curated Lens — "All Events, One Voice" (Recommended)

**What it looks like:** Cover all event types in Madison, but curate and present them through a specific editorial voice. Think of it as "what your most interesting friend in Madison would tell you to do this weekend."

**The voice:** Informed, a little irreverent, taste-forward. Knows the difference between a tourist trap and a local gem. Gets equally excited about a trail run and an underground DJ set. Brings the same energy to a food truck rally as a gallery opening. Tech-literate without being tech-bro cringe.

**What makes it different from 608today:**
- 608today tells you *what* is happening. You tell people *what's worth doing*.
- 608today covers 15 events in a flat list. You pick 5 and tell people why.
- 608today is a news digest. You're an opinionated friend.

**Pros:**
- Full addressable market (anyone who goes to events in Madison)
- Clear differentiation from every competitor (none have editorial voice)
- Personality drives opens, shares, and loyalty — newsletter subscribers follow *voice*
- Broad sponsor base (any business targeting active, taste-driven 25-45 year olds)
- Higher CPMs than generic ($30-50) because the audience is defined by psychographics, not just geography
- You'd actually enjoy reading and building it
- Personal utility built in: the curation process IS you finding events you want to attend

**Cons:**
- Requires editorial judgment (can't be fully automated — but that's also the moat)
- More work per issue than a dump-all-events approach
- Brand takes time to develop (voice emerges over 10-20 issues)

**Revenue ceiling:** $8-15K/month at maturity. 5-10K subscribers at $15-20 ARPU through premium sponsor positioning. The audience quality commands premium rates.

## Why Option C Wins

### The Math

| Metric | A (Niche) | B (Broad) | C (Curated) |
|--------|-----------|-----------|--------------|
| Addressable audience | 15-30K | 200K+ | 100K+ |
| Realistic sub ceiling | 3-5K | 5-8K* | 8-15K |
| CPM range | $50-70 | $11-15 | $30-50 |
| Sponsor pool | Narrow | Wide | Wide + premium |
| Monthly rev at maturity | $3-5K | $0-15K | $8-15K |
| Defensibility | Medium | None | High |
| Personal enjoyment | High | Low | High |

*\*Against 608today, you'd struggle to reach even this.*

### The Positioning Statement

> **Madison Events** is the opinionated guide to what's actually worth doing in Madison. We sift through hundreds of events so you don't have to — and we tell you which ones are worth your time.

### The Brand Personality

- **Not** a calendar. A curator.
- **Not** comprehensive. Selective.
- **Not** neutral. Opinionated (but not mean).
- **Not** tourist-facing. Local-first.
- Comfortable recommending a Terrace sunset *and* a warehouse techno night.
- Knows that the best things in Madison often aren't on Eventbrite.

## How Personal Taste Fits In

This is the key insight: **your taste IS the product, not a limitation on it**.

The categories you naturally gravitate toward (outdoor, music, tech) become **recurring sections** or **lenses**, not filters that exclude everything else:

| Your instinct | How it shows up |
|---------------|-----------------|
| Outdoor/active | "Trail of the Week" or "Get Outside" section |
| Unique music taste | "What We're Listening To" — a curated pick that isn't the obvious headliner |
| Tech scene | "Madison Builds" — tech meetups, startup events, hackathons |
| General taste | Main picks are just the best 5-7 events of the week, any category |

This way:
- Someone who only cares about music still subscribes (the picks are great)
- Someone who only wants weekend family activities still subscribes (you cover those too, through the same lens)
- Sponsors see a defined, quality audience (not a random dump)
- You build something you're proud of

## Implementation Roadmap

### Phase 1: Voice Development (Weeks 1-3)
1. Write 3 sample newsletter editions with the curated voice
2. Pick a name/brand that signals curation (options below)
3. Set up Beehiiv account (free tier: 2,500 subs)
4. Create a simple landing page with the positioning statement
5. Publish to social channels for initial subscriber acquisition

### Phase 2: Launch & Iterate (Weeks 4-8)
1. Send weekly newsletter every Wednesday or Thursday (weekend planning)
2. Maintain the automated scraper pipeline for raw event data
3. Add manual curation layer: pick 5-7 highlights + write commentary
4. Establish recurring sections (Get Outside, Sound Check, etc.)
5. Target: 200-500 subscribers

### Phase 3: Monetize (Weeks 8-16)
1. At 500 subs: Enable Beehiiv ad network
2. At 1,000 subs: Approach first local sponsors (breweries, outdoor shops, venues)
3. Introduce "Featured Event" placement for paying venues ($25-50/feature)
4. Target: 1,000-2,500 subscribers, first $500/month in revenue

### Phase 4: Scale (Months 4-8)
1. Add a second weekly send (Monday "This Week" preview)
2. Launch referral program (Beehiiv built-in)
3. Approach premium sponsors (Epic Systems, American Family, local tech companies)
4. Target: 2,500-5,000 subscribers, $2-3K/month

## What Changes in the Existing Codebase

The current product is built for Option B (comprehensive aggregation). To support Option C:

| Component | Current State | What Changes |
|-----------|--------------|--------------|
| **Scrapers** | Pull everything | Keep as-is — raw data feeds the curation process |
| **Website** | Flat event list | Add "Editor's Picks" section at top, category tags |
| **Newsletter** | Auto-generated dump | Needs manual curation layer + editorial commentary |
| **Social** | Template-based | Needs voice rewrite — personality in every post |
| **Config** | Generic tagline | Update brand name, tagline, positioning |
| **New: Curation tool** | Doesn't exist | Simple CLI or web UI to select & annotate picks from scraped events |

The scrapers and data pipeline remain valuable — they give you the raw material to curate from. The main addition is an editorial layer on top.

## Key Risk: The 6AM City Factor

6AM City acquired Good Daily (AI newsletter startup) in July 2025 and is scaling to 350+ markets with AI-generated content. Their Madison newsletter (608today) has 33K subscribers.

**Why this is actually good for you:**
- They're optimizing for breadth (markets) not depth (quality per market)
- AI-generated content is their competitive advantage — but also their ceiling. It'll never have genuine local voice.
- Their $10 ARPU benchmark means they need massive scale. You can be profitable at 2,500 subs with $20 ARPU.
- They validate the market. People in Madison want a local newsletter. You just need to be the better one.

**The risk:**
- If they decide to invest heavily in Madison specifically, they could out-resource you
- Mitigation: voice and community can't be bought. Build relationships, not just a list.

The recommendation is **Option C: Curated Lens**. It's the only option that:
1. Lets you build something you'd personally use and enjoy
2. Has a defensible market position against well-funded competitors
3. Can realistically generate meaningful revenue ($5-15K/month)
4. Turns your personal taste into an asset rather than a limitation
